Fig. 5 — Complement: x → 1−x

What this diagram shows: Complement is the simplest of the three involutions. It requires only a single line with two marked endpoints — 0 and 1 — and a compass to bisect the segment between them. The midpoint ½ is the fixed point: it maps to itself. Every other point x in the interval maps to 1−x, its mirror image across ½.

The compass arcs from 0 and 1 (equal radius, slightly greater than half the unit) intersect above the line. The vertical dropped from that intersection locates ½ by pure bisection — no ratio, no multiplication, no second axis. This is an entirely additive construction: it measures and bisects a distance.

The equal distance markers show that x and 1−x are always the same distance from ½ — one to the left, one to the right. The swap is a reflection.

Minimal apparatus: one line, two marked points (0 and 1), a compass. No multiplication, no ratio, no second axis. The unit is treated as a length: the distance from 0 to 1.
